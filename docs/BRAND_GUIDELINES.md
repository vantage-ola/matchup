# Matchup — Brand Guidelines

> Version 1.0 · Internal Use · Not for distribution

---

## 01. Brand Name

**Matchup**

One word. No camel case. No hyphen. No tagline appended to the wordmark.

When written in full prose, it is always **Matchup** — capital M, lowercase u. Never MATCHUP, never Match Up, never match-up. In UI contexts where all-caps treatment is used for labels or headers, the brand name is excluded from that treatment.

The name carries two meanings simultaneously: the head-to-head contest between two players, and the pairing of your game against the real one. Both readings are intentional.

---

## 02. Tagline

**Play the match. Own the result.**

This is the primary tagline. It does one job: it tells you that something active is happening here — not watching, not predicting, not betting. Playing.

**Secondary line (for longer contexts):**
*Your moves. Their moves. One real match to settle it.*

The secondary line is used in onboarding copy, app store descriptions, and pitch decks where more explanation is warranted. It should never appear alongside the primary tagline — pick one for any given context.

## 03. Positioning

### The One-Line Position
Matchup is the first game where your football skill — not your luck, not your loyalty — determines what you walk away with.

### The Market Gap
There is a line between watching football and participating in it. On one side: passive consumption — streaming, group chats, reaction memes. On the other side: fantasy leagues that require spreadsheet thinking, or bet slips that reduce everything to luck. Matchup lives in the white space between those two things. It is participation without passivity. It is competition without randomness.

### Who It's For
Matchup is built for the Nigerian football fan — 18 to 30 — who knows the game deeply and has never found a product that respects that knowledge. The fan who calls the lineup before it's announced. The one who would rather be right for the right reasons than win by accident. Mobile-first. Banter-native. Already inside football culture, already frustrated that nothing on the market speaks to them without condescension.

This person does not need another loyalty points programme or a beginner's tutorial. They need a real opponent, a real match, and a real game between the two.

### The Competitive Distinction
Matchup is not a betting app. It is not a fantasy league. It is not a football management sim. It is a head-to-head skill game that uses a real football match as its settlement layer. The distinction is not legal caution — it is the truth of what the product is. Skill determines the outcome. The real match determines the context. These are different things and both matter.

## 04. Brand Pillars

These four pillars are the load-bearing walls of the brand. Every product decision, communication choice, and design direction either reinforces them or it doesn't. If it doesn't, it doesn't ship.

---

### Pillar 1 — Skill Over Luck

The core promise. Everything in Matchup — the move system, the simultaneous reveal, the proportional payout — exists to ensure that the better player walks away ahead, not the luckier one. This is what separates Matchup from a bet slip at the deepest level.

In communications this shows up as: directness over hype, earned outcomes over instant gratification, respecting the user's intelligence. We never promise a jackpot. We promise a fair fight.

---

### Pillar 2 — Participation Over Consumption

Football already has spectators. Matchup creates players. The entire product experience is built around turning passive watching into active doing — giving the group chat guy somewhere to put his conviction besides a tweet.

In communications this shows up as: active verbs, not passive ones. "Play the match" not "watch more football." We never position Matchup as a companion to watching — we position it as the thing you do instead of just watching.

---

### Pillar 3 — Clarity Over Complexity

The UI should be immediate. The rules should be graspable in one session. The settlement screen should be readable in five seconds. Complexity is a failure state, not a feature. If something needs a tutorial longer than thirty seconds, it probably shouldn't be in the product.

In communications this shows up as: short sentences. Plain language. No jargon from the worlds of betting, gaming, or finance unless it's football jargon — which our user already speaks fluently. We don't explain what a press is. We explain what happens when yours doesn't work.

---

### Pillar 4 — Authenticity to Football Culture

Matchup was built by people who watch football the way our users watch football — anxiously, emotionally, tribally. The product is not a football-themed wrapper on a card game. It is a football product, in the same way that FM is a football product. The vocabulary, the rhythm, the tension — all of it comes from the sport, not from the app ecosystem.

In communications this shows up as: real match references, real emotions, the specific language of Nigerian football fandom. Not sanitised, not corporate, not translated for a general audience. This product is for one audience and it knows it.

## 05. Voice and Tone

### The Voice

Matchup speaks like someone who is very good at football and is not trying to convince you of that. Confident without performance. Knowledgeable without showing off. The friend in the group chat who says "watch, Salah scores here" and is right — and says nothing when he is.

The voice is consistent across every context. What changes is the tone.

---

### Tone Across Contexts

**In marketing copy:**
Sharp. Punchy. Slightly provocative. We are allowed to take a position. We are allowed to say that what exists now isn't good enough. We are not allowed to be smug about it.

*Example:* "A bet slip doesn't care how much you know. We do."

**In product UI:**
Minimal and functional. The interface speaks only when it has to, and when it does, it is clear and direct. No cheerful loading messages. No congratulatory language that feels pasted from a fintech app. When a user wins, we tell them what happened and how much they earned — the game itself is the celebration.

*Example:* "You won the Matchup. +₦566 credited." Not: "🎉 Congratulations! You're a MatchUp champion!"

**In onboarding:**
Patient but not slow. We assume the user is intelligent. We explain the things that are genuinely new — the simultaneous move reveal, the settlement layer — and we trust the user to understand football without explaining it to them.

*Example:* "Both players commit a move at the same time. Neither of you sees what the other picked — until you both have. That's the game."

**In error states:**
Honest and human. We do not blame the user and we do not pretend nothing happened. If a match is delayed, we say so. If a payout is pending, we say why. The trust we ask for is proportional to the transparency we give.

*Example:* "The real match result hasn't come through yet. We're checking every few minutes — you'll get a notification the moment it does."

**In settlement:**
This is the most important moment in the product. The tone here is calm and clear — like a final whistle, not a fanfare. Show the numbers. Show the comparison. Let the result speak.

---

### What the Voice Is Never

- **Hype-inflated.** We do not use "MASSIVE," "EPIC," "INSANE" as default modifiers. If something is genuinely extraordinary, the facts make the case — we do not amplify them with adjectives.

- **Apologetic.** We do not hedge our positioning. We do not write "kind of like a game" or "a bit like football but simpler." We say what we are.

- **Condescending.** We never explain what a yellow card is. We never assume our users need football demystified.

- **Generic fintech or gaming.** We are not a wallet app with a football skin. We do not say "seamless experience," "level up," "unlock rewards," or "your journey." These words describe other products. Matchup uses football language.

## 06. Core Message

Everything Matchup communicates — in a press release, an ad, an onboarding screen, a tweet — is a version of one message:

> **Football rewards knowledge. So does Matchup.**

Every other message is a branch off this trunk. The skill system branches from it. The proportional payout branches from it. The decision to not be a bet slip branches from it. When a piece of copy doesn't trace back to this message, it does not represent the brand.

---

## 07. What Matchup Is Not — A Usage Guardrail

This section exists to prevent brand drift. As the product grows, every new feature, partnership, and communication will pull the brand in a direction. This is where we document the directions we are not going.

**Matchup is not a gambling product.** We do not use the language of gambling — odds, punts, accumulators, winnings. We are a skill game with a payout. The payout is not a prize — it is the result of better play. The language we use must maintain this distinction at all times.

**Matchup is not for everyone.** We do not chase the general public. We do not write for people who don't watch football. We do not simplify or de-football the product to expand the addressable market. The depth of the audience's football knowledge is the product's most important feature — we protect it.

**Matchup is not a social network.** We do not build for sharing, following, or public performance. Matchup is about the head-to-head. Profiles, leaderboards, and social graphs are secondary surfaces at best. The game is between two people and a real match — that triangle is the whole product.

**Matchup is not built for addiction.** We do not engineer compulsive return. The product has a natural clock — fixtures, kickoffs, settlements. We lean into that natural rhythm instead of manufacturing artificial urgency. No daily streaks. No spin-the-wheel mechanics. The match will always be there. So will we.
